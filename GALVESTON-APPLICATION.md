```
Mengzhen Yang (b. 1993, Ma'anshan, Anhui, China) is a visual artist based in Los Angeles. Her practice centers on oil painting — large-scale, physically demanding works built through aggressive impasto and palette-knife techniques over months and years. Her painting "I Am a Growth" took over three years of continuous creation.

Yang's work spans oil painting, charcoal drawing, watercolor studies, digital illustration, and generative art — unified by an uncompromising visual language she calls "Death Pencil": raw, confrontational, and rooted in the tension between control and chaos. Her subjects range from figurative portraits to animal archetypes to dense, jungle-like abstract compositions where color and material assert their own agency.

She has built a body of over 20 works across multiple series, including the Crimson Sovereign oil paintings, the Portrait Glitch series merging hand-drawn charcoal with digital disruption, and immersive botanical compositions exploring growth, descent, and transformation.
```

## 2. Artist Statement（复制粘贴）

```
I paint because language fails. Every painting I make is an attempt to hold something that words dissolve — the weight of growth, the violence of color, the silence inside chaos.

My primary medium is oil on canvas, applied with palette knife and bare physicality. I do not sketch first. I begin with color and force, and the image reveals itself through accumulation — layer over layer, session over session, sometimes over years. "I Am a Growth" took three years. I almost slept on the street with it. It became a living thing.

My recent work explores two poles: the animal and the garden. In "Crimson Sovereign," a fox emerges from explosive reds and yellows — not illustrated but excavated from the paint itself. In "Jungle Descent," vines and flowers cascade through deep blue darkness, with ladders and dripping white paint creating vertical pull — a world that is growing and falling simultaneously.

I also maintain a Portrait Glitch series where hand-drawn charcoal faces are disrupted by digital overlays — halos, dollar signs, pixel fragments — collapsing identity into symbol. And I write generative code that translates the gestural energy of painting into algorithmic process.

But the center is always the canvas. The body. The knife. The hours.
```

## 3. Statement of Intent（驻留目的，复制粘贴）

```
At Galveston, I want to do what I do best: paint. Large. Slowly. Physically.

My practice demands sustained, uninterrupted time with my materials. A single painting can take months of daily work — building impasto layers, scraping back, rebuilding. The 500 sq ft studio and ten-month duration at GAR would allow me to develop a new body of large-scale oil paintings that I cannot execute in my current conditions.

Specifically, I plan to create a new series of 4-6 monumental paintings (each 120-180 cm) exploring the threshold between figuration and abstraction — continuing the trajectory of "Crimson Sovereign" and "Jungle Descent." These works investigate the moment when a recognizable form (an animal, a body, a garden) dissolves into pure material: color, texture, gravity, drip. I want to push the physical limits of oil paint — thicker, heavier, more sculptural.

Galveston's coastal environment is directly relevant: the humidity, salt air, and Gulf light will influence drying times and surface qualities in ways I want to explore. The isolation and focus of a residency community — three artists working in parallel — offers exactly the sustained concentration my process requires.

I would also use the residency to prepare a cohesive portfolio and exhibition proposal from this new body of work, positioning it for gallery representation and international exhibition opportunities.
```
